Differential Equations

When a rate of change talks back to the original function, we are inside a differential equation. These relationships let us model growth, motion, mixing, and any system where change depends on the current state.

In this lesson, you will read slope fields like topography, sketch solutions from an initial condition, and reason about why certain forms are separable or linear.

Core Concept Cards

Separable

\(\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)h(y)\)

A differential equation you can split into a pure \(x\) side and a pure \(y\) side.

\(\int \frac{1}{h(y)} dy = \int g(x) dx\)

Equilibrium

\(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\)

A constant solution where the slope field is flat.

Example: \(y = 0\) solves \(y' = y\).

Direction Field

\(f(x,y)\)

A map of local slopes that hints at solution shapes.

Each small segment points in the direction of change.

Exercises

Find the general solution.

\(x^2 + 4 - y^3\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\)

\(x^2 + 4 = y^3\frac{dy}{dx}\) (rearrange terms)
\((x^2+4)\,dx = y^3\,dy\) (separate variables)
\(\frac{1}{3}x^3 + 4x = \frac{1}{4}y^4 + C\) (integrate both sides)
\(4x^3 + 48x - 3y^4 = C\) (combine constants)

Problem sourced from Emory Oxford Math Center

Solve the differential equation with the given condition.

\(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{5x^2y^2 + y^2}{x^2y^5 + 4x^2},\quad y(1)=2\)

\(\frac{y^2(5x^2+1)}{x^2(y^5+4)}\) (factor numerator and denominator)
\(\frac{y^5+4}{y^2} dy = \frac{5x^2+1}{x^2} dx\) (separate variables)
\(\int (y^3 + 4y^{-2})\,dy = \int (5 + x^{-2})\,dx\) (rewrite with powers)
\(\frac{1}{4}y^4 - \frac{4}{y} = 5x - \frac{1}{x} + C\) (integrate)
\(C = -2\) (apply \(y(1)=2\))

Problem sourced from Emory Oxford Math Center

A cup of coffee starts at \(100^\circ\text{C}\) in a \(20^\circ\text{C}\) room. After 4 minutes, it has cooled to \(90^\circ\text{C}\). Using Newton's Law of Cooling, find \(T(t)\), the temperature at time \(t\).

\(\frac{dT}{dt} = k(T-20)\) (Newton's Law of Cooling)
\(\frac{dT}{T-20} = k\,dt\) (separate variables)
\(\ln|T-20| = kt + C\) (integrate)
\(T = 20 + Ae^{kt}\) (exponentiate)
\(A = 80\) (apply \(T(0)=100\))
\(k = \frac{1}{4}\ln\left(\frac{7}{8}\right)\) (apply \(T(4)=90\))
\(T(t)=20+80e^{\frac{t}{4}\ln(7/8)}\) (final model)

Closure

  • Direction fields encode the local story of a differential equation.
  • Initial conditions pick out a single solution curve from infinitely many.
  • Separable equations turn change into an integral on each side.
